History of Computer

Introduction

The history of computer focuses on the physical devices (hardware) built to process data. It traces their evolution from early mechanical calculators to powerful modern-day machines that drive global communication, science, and business. The evolution of computer is broken down into five (5) Generations, following advancements in computer circuits system. Each generation brought massive improvements in size, speed, efficiency and reliability.


1. First Generation Computers (1940 – 1956) - Vacuum Tubes

Description:

The circuitry relied on large glass tubes (vacuum tubes) to control electric current. They acted as switches and amplifiers. Circuits were wired manually and consumed massive power.

Vacuum Tubes.

Advantages:

i. Made fully electronic computing possible

ii. Faster than mechanical relays

Limitations:

i. Huge size and generated a lot of heat

ii. Frequent failures and short lifespan

iii. Very high power consumption

ENIAC-1946 (1st Gen. Computer)

2. Second Generation Computers (1956 – 1963) - Transistors

Description:

Vacuum tubes were replaced by transistors (semiconductor devices), which performed the same switching function but were much smaller, more efficient, and reliable. - Smaller and more energy-efficient.

Transistors.

Advantages:

i. Smaller and more energy-efficient

ii. Less heat and longer life

iii. Faster switching speed

Limitations:

i. Still required complex manual wiring

ii. Limited integration (single transistors)

IBM-1620 (2nd Gen. Computer)

3. Third Generation Computers (1964 – 1971) - Integrated Circuits (ICs)

Description:

Many transistors and electronic components were integrated into a single silicon chip, reducing size and increasing reliability and speed. - Dramatic reduction in size

Integrated Circuit (IC)

Advantages:

i. Dramatic reduction in size

ii. Much faster and more reliable

iii. Lower power usage and easier mass production

Limitations:

i. Expensive early development

ii. Complex fabrication process

IBM System/360 (3rd Gen. Computer)

4. Fourth Generation Computers (1971 – 1989) - Microprocessors

Description:

Thousands to millions of transistors were integrated into a single chip, forming a complete CPU. Circuits became highly compact and powerful.

Integrated Circuit (VLSI)

Advantages:

i. Extremely small and powerful

ii. Mass production lowered cost

iii. Enabled personal computers

Limitations:

i. Complex fabrication process

ii. Heat dissipation challenges with increasing transistor density

Hp Desktop Computer (4th Gen. Computer)

5. Fifth Generation Computers (1990 – Present) - ULSI, AI and Beyond

Description:

Circuitry now integrates billions of transistors on a single chip. Specialized circuits and quantum circuits in research labs push limits further

AI Chip

Advantages:

i. Unprecedented processing power

ii. Low power per operation

iii. Supports AI, parallelism, and quantum computing

Limitations:

i. Expensive and complex fabrication

ii. Quantum systems still experimental

Intelligent System (Future of Computer)

Summary:

The circuitry system is the backbone of computer evolution. It transformed from the bulky, fragile vacuum tubes to ultra-dense, intelligent microchips and experimental quantum circuits.
